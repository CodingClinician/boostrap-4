About Autocross

Keeping the racing real

Autocross is a form of motorsport where a course is laid out with traffic cones in a big paved surface. Drivers navigate through the course one at a time and attempt to drive through the course as fast as possible, without hitting any of the cones on the course. Hitting a cone usually results in a two second time penalty that is added to the driver's total time for a given run. Not going through the series of cones as mandated by the organizers results in a "Did Not Finish (DNF) " where the time for that run is voided.

This form of motorsport aims to challenge a driver's ability in controlling his or her car through the course. and is focused more on car control as oppose to outright speed and car engine power. Autocross allows a driver to hone and master car control skills that can translate to other forms of timed motorsports competition. It is a legitimate way to explore and improve your driving capabilities. It is often noted that autocross is the gateway to other more involved forms of timed motorsports. But the real best part about participating in autocross are the people that you meet in the sport. For the most part, these people are car enthusiasts that just likes to have fun with their car in a legal way. And these people are also some of the nicest people you will ever meet.

A properly sanctioned autocross event is legal in regards to the violation of common traffic laws. The events are held in controlled environments that mitigates the risks to the participants and their vehicles. Along with this safety protocols are in place during the event. The New York Region SCCA follows the mandates set forth by the Sports Car Club of America for safely holding an autocross event.

Safety protocols are in place in accordance with the guidelines set forth by the Sports Car Club of America. As a new participant, it is recommended that at minimum you attend the driver's meeting and participate in at least 1 novice walk. Safety protocols are reviewed at the driver's meeting and the novice walk is an opportunity for a beginner to learn the basics of navigating through an autocross course. When driving on course, pay attention to red flags being waved on course. When you see a red flag being waved, come to a controlled full stop. you will be given a re run if there is an emergent situation on course while you where taking a run.

Anybody that holds a currently issued valid driver's license with a car that is deemed safe to be driven on course by the event organizers.

Every vehicle that will go into the course MUST pass a technical inspection for safety and course worthiness.

Working an event is part of participating in an autocross. Every single participant is given a specifc work assignment before, during and after the event. The autocross organizers of the event do the work on a voluntary basis based on their love for cars and performance driving. The entry fee that a participant pays goes towards the rental of the autocross site, maintenance and procurement of club equipment required to hold an autocross event, and administrative costs related to holding an event. Competitor participation in the event is one of the key factos that keeps the cost of autocross participation very low in comparison with other forms of timed sanctioned motorsports events. Work assignments and run group assignments are announced at the driver's meeting before the event starts. Essentially for the day, you're either "driving" or "working".

Station work

Setting up knocked down cones, calling in penalties to timing and scoring, and flagging.

Grid

Preparing drivers to take their runs, lining them up to the start, marking the run sticker.

Timing and scoring

Managing the timing software, keeping track of driver penalties, communicating to grid which driver gets a re run.

When to call or NOT call in a cone penalty for a driver

  • Battery safely anchored in a proper battery mount.
  • Seatbelts can be either factory installed or properly installed after market units.
  • Ball joints, MUST NOT have excessive play.
  • Wheels MUST be properly mounted to the axles with a wheel nut present in EACH wheel stud.
  • Absolutely NO leaking fluids.
  • A proper exhaust system that DOES NOT exceed the 90 db sound limit and is properly secured to the vehicle.
  • Wheels must be properly mounted to the axles with a wheel nut present in EVERY wheel stud.
  • NO floormats, these MUST be taken off.
  • NO loose items inside the care that may move during suddedn cornering.

    • The above are just general guidelines, individual issues could be addressed with the tech inspection team.

    The event organizers have the final decision if a vehicle is suited for competition driving at ANY given event.

  • Refer to the tech inspection guide
  • Bring a roll of painters tape for your numbers, DO NOT USE duct tape, it will ruin your paint!
  • Use a contrasting color tape to the color of your car.
  • Pre register for the event if you can.
  • Come as early as you can to the event
  • When you arrive at the site, empty out your car.
  • Spare tire, tools, or any other personal belonings MUST be out of the car to pass tech inspection
  • Numbers must be MINIMUM 8 inches tall on the SIDE of your car.
  • Go through tech inspection as soon as you could.
  • Attend the novice walk.
  • Attend the drivers meeting, it is mandatory for EVERYONE.
  • Pay attention to when you should be working or driving.>
  • If you are working, check in to the tent before proceeding to your work station.
  • If you've never autocrossed before, ask for an instructor on your first few runs.
  • Observe the 5 mph speed limit in grid and paddock. Once you're "Off the course" this restriction comes into play.

    • Stay safe and have fun. Having fun is what an autocross is all about.
